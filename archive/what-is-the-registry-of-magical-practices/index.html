Registry File

What Is the Registry of Magical Practices?

The quiet institution behind The Monstrous Beloved is less a villain than a system with very sharp teeth.

By Selena Monroe · May 12, 2026 · 6 minute read

Every fantasy world has laws. Some are written into spellbooks, some into old bargains, and some into the quiet habits people inherit without noticing. In The Monstrous Beloved, the Registry of Magical Practices is where those laws become paperwork, surveillance, sealed records, and decisions that follow people long after the original danger has passed.

The Registry is not meant to be a castle full of obvious villains. It is more unsettling than that. It is an institution staffed by people who can believe they are doing necessary work while still preserving harm, burying inconvenient evidence, and deciding that certain lives are easier to manage when they are reduced to a classification.

A System Built to Name the Unruly

The Registry exists because magic is real, monsters are real, and human communities have always tried to survive what they do not fully understand. In theory, its purpose is order: identify magical practices, regulate dangerous work, document non-human beings, and prevent disaster before it spreads. That kind of institution can sound reasonable from a distance.

But naming a thing is never neutral. A classification decides what counts as evidence. It decides who is believed. It decides whether a creature is treated as a person, a threat, a resource, or an impossible exception that the record cannot accommodate. Once a file exists, it can protect someone. It can also become the cage that follows them.

Why the Registry Matters to the Series

The Monstrous Beloved is a six-book sapphic dark romantasy series about women and monsters choosing each other under pressure. Each book stands on its own, with its own central relationship and its own gothic texture, but the Registry is the thread running beneath them. It is background at first. Then the pattern starts to show.

A failed summoning does not stay private. A coastal archive has gaps where truth should be. A supposedly extinct creature has survived outside the official record. A broker of forbidden components understands the magical world more accurately than the institution sworn to regulate it. Every book asks a version of the same question: who benefits when the record is wrong?

The Horror of Being Misfiled

For Selena Monroe, monsters are rarely frightening because they are unknowable. They are frightening because someone else has already decided what they are allowed to mean. Demon, selkie, strix, entity, shifter, vampire: each word carries a history. The Registry turns that history into policy.

That makes the archive one of the series' central gothic spaces. Not every locked room has a door. Some are made of redactions, procedural language, and records no one is supposed to request. The horror is not only that a file exists. It is that the file may be incomplete, convenient, or old enough that everyone has mistaken it for truth.

Where to Start

If you are new to The Monstrous Beloved, begin with The Binding Night. The Registry appears there through Victor Marlow, whose official role brings the wider institution to the edge of Sable and Vex's impossible bond. From there, each book widens the view. The Registry is not the romance. It is the pressure system the romances have to survive.
