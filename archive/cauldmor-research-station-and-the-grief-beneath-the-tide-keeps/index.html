Cauldmor Research Station and the Grief Beneath The Tide Keeps

The coast in The Tide Keeps is not a backdrop. It is an archive, a wound, and a witness.

By Selena Monroe · June 2, 2026 · 6 minute read

The Tide Keeps belongs to the coast. Not a bright, postcard coast, but a headland shaped by weather, rock, salt, and the kind of isolation that makes every missing record feel deliberate. Cauldmor Research Station is where Petra arrives to rebuild a career in quiet. It is also where Aoibhinn has been waiting on the rocks below, carrying grief the official archive cannot explain.

A research station is a useful gothic setting because it promises objectivity. Measurements, surveys, specimens, reports: everything should be documented. When something is absent from that kind of place, the absence starts to speak.

Archive Gaps as Haunting

Haunting does not always require footsteps in an empty hall. Sometimes it is a report that stops mid-sentence. Sometimes it is a survey that should have been completed and was not. Sometimes it is a pattern of professional silence around a coastline everyone knows has a history, even if they disagree about what that history means.

For Petra, Cauldmor is supposed to be a controlled environment where she can do careful work and become useful again. Instead, the station gives her gaps. Those gaps are not decorative. They are evidence of a world where institutions can make harm harder to name by deciding what belongs in the record.

Why Selkies Carry So Much Narrative Weight

Selkie stories are already built around questions of possession, return, and the body as a site of freedom. A skin is never just an object. It is selfhood, mobility, danger, memory, and the possibility of leaving. In The Tide Keeps, that mythology becomes a way to write about grief without making grief passive.

Aoibhinn is not waiting because she has no agency. She is waiting because grief can make a life narrow around a single place, a single decision, a single version of the past. The romance asks what it means to be seen by someone who does not try to turn that grief into a tidy lesson.

The Registry at the Waterline

The Registry's presence in The Tide Keeps is quieter than a confrontation and colder than a monster in the dark. It exists in sealed decisions, denied provenance, and the way institutional power can outlast the people who made the first choice. That makes Cauldmor part setting and part indictment.

The station's scientific language matters because it rubs against the older language of the coast. Tide, skin, witness, evidence, grief: none of these words stays in only one category. That friction is where the book lives.

A Coastal Gothic Love Story

At its heart, The Tide Keeps is still a romance. Petra and Aoibhinn are not metaphors talking to each other on a rock. They are two people with different relationships to loss, knowledge, and the possibility of choosing the future without pretending the past has been resolved. The coast keeps what happened. The question is whether it can also hold what comes next.
